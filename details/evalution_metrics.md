# Six Evaluation Axes

## Answer 1: Definition of the Six Axes for Evaluating AI Builder Candidates

When the resume is the main input, the evaluation should not depend too heavily on surface-level signals such as school name, company brand, job title, or years of experience. Those signals can disadvantage candidates from diverse or non-traditional backgrounds. The six axes should focus on what the candidate has built, what problems they solved, how they approached the work, and whether they show the judgment needed for an AI Builder role.

Each axis is scored from 0 to 10. The score should never appear alone. It should always be paired with qualitative evidence from the resume. In the final dashboard, the six scores can be visualized as a radar chart or hexagon chart. When a reviewer selects an axis, they should be able to see the extracted evidence, uncertainty, and follow-up questions for human review.

## Axis 1. Problem Framing and Ambiguity Handling

**Definition:** The ability to structure an ambiguous or open-ended problem and narrow it into a practical scope.

An AI Builder is not only someone who implements given requirements. They also need to identify what is worth building in unclear business contexts. In the resume, this axis looks for evidence of strategy, problem definition, discovery, stakeholder alignment, product scoping, consulting projects, and research-to-product translation.

**High-score signals:**

- Turned an ambiguous business problem into a product, automation, model, workflow, or prototype
- Built and tested assumptions when requirements were incomplete
- Defined scope, success criteria, or user needs directly
- Structured problems across consulting, product, research, operations, or process-improvement contexts

**Low-score signals:**

- Mainly executed predefined tasks without visible problem-definition work
- Listed technical tools without explaining why the work mattered
- Described outputs without explaining what problem they solved

**0-10 scoring guide:**

- 0-2: Little or no visible problem-framing experience
- 3-5: Some project experience, but mostly task execution based on given requirements
- 6-8: Clear evidence of structuring problems and proposing solution directions
- 9-10: Strong evidence of setting direction and executing through ambiguity with complex stakeholders

## Axis 2. Practical Building and Execution

**Definition:** The ability to turn an idea into a working artifact.

The AI Builder role requires more than describing concepts. A strong candidate should be able to build prototypes, tools, workflows, dashboards, APIs, automations, agents, or systems that can be inspected and tested. This axis looks for evidence of apps, models, agents, dashboards, APIs, workflows, internal tools, MVPs, and production deployment.

**High-score signals:**

- Built usable products, tools, automations, data pipelines, or AI workflows
- Moved from prototype to a more operational or production-like system
- Created tangible output under time or resource constraints
- Provided inspectable artifacts such as GitHub repositories, demos, deployed apps, case studies, or measurable results

**Low-score signals:**

- Mostly focused on meetings, research, or strategy documents with little implementation evidence
- Listed technologies without clear finished outputs
- Ran experiments that were not connected to users or business value

**0-10 scoring guide:**

- 0-2: Little or no hands-on build experience
- 3-5: Some learning projects or limited project experience, but weak real-use context
- 6-8: Multiple examples of working tools or systems
- 9-10: Strong evidence of turning complex problems into artifacts used by real users or organizations

## Axis 3. AI Leverage and Technical Fluency

**Definition:** The ability to use AI tools, models, prompts, data, and automation in a purposeful way, while understanding their limits.

An AI Builder does not need to be a PhD-level ML researcher. However, they should be able to apply AI in real workflows rather than using AI as a buzzword. This axis looks for LLM, RAG, agent, prompt engineering, evaluation, automation, model integration, API usage, analytics, and AI governance experience.

**High-score signals:**

- Used LLMs or AI APIs to improve a real workflow
- Designed RAG, agents, prompt evaluation, model comparison, or human review loops
- Mentioned AI limits such as hallucination, evaluation, or data quality
- Connected AI use to user or business problems rather than treating AI as the goal itself

**Low-score signals:**

- Used many AI keywords without concrete examples
- Only showed personal productivity use of ChatGPT or similar tools
- Did not show awareness of model evaluation or risk management

**0-10 scoring guide:**

- 0-2: Little or no AI usage experience
- 3-5: Personal productivity or simple experimentation with AI
- 6-8: Applied AI to products, workflow automation, analysis, or decision-support processes
- 9-10: Designed, evaluated, operated, or helped scale AI systems in an organizational context

## Axis 4. Enterprise Judgment and Governance

**Definition:** The ability to consider security, privacy, risk, scalability, operations, and governance in an enterprise environment.

In an organization like KPMG, building quickly is important, but building safely is just as important. This axis looks for experience in regulated industries, security, privacy, compliance, risk management, auditability, deployment processes, MLOps, DevOps, and stakeholder approvals.

**High-score signals:**

- Considered security, privacy, compliance, audit, or governance in project work
- Built solutions in enterprise or complex organizational environments
- Considered operations, maintainability, scalability, monitoring, or documentation
- Balanced speed of experimentation with risk management

**Low-score signals:**

- Many personal projects or experiments, but weak enterprise applicability
- No mention of data security, privacy, or governance
- No visible production or operational experience

**0-10 scoring guide:**

- 0-2: Little or no evidence of enterprise risk awareness
- 3-5: Some operational experience, but weak governance perspective
- 6-8: Clear enterprise experience involving operations, security, or cross-functional collaboration
- 9-10: Strong evidence of balancing innovation and risk in complex enterprise settings

## Axis 5. Stakeholder and User-Centered Thinking

**Definition:** The ability to understand and balance the needs of users, business leaders, operations teams, technical teams, risk teams, and other stakeholders.

An AI Builder does not build models in isolation. They need to create solutions that real people can use. This axis looks for user research, client-facing work, workshops, consulting, cross-functional collaboration, product management, and change management experience.

**High-score signals:**

- Worked with non-technical users or gathered requirements from stakeholders
- Built products or workflows with multiple teams
- Supported user adoption, training, documentation, or feedback loops
- Considered both business value and user experience

**Low-score signals:**

- Strong technical implementation, but unclear target users
- Mostly solo projects with limited collaboration context
- No evidence of handling stakeholder conflict or trade-offs

**0-10 scoring guide:**

- 0-2: Little or no user or stakeholder perspective
- 3-5: Some collaboration experience, but limited stakeholder ownership
- 6-8: Clear experience working with stakeholders and translating needs into product decisions
- 9-10: Strong evidence of creating alignment and driving adoption in complex stakeholder environments

## Axis 6. Communication, Learning Agility, and Evidence of Growth

**Definition:** The ability to explain decisions, results, limitations, and learning clearly, while adapting quickly to new domains.

AI Builder candidates can come from many backgrounds. Therefore, learning speed and communication matter more than a single academic or career path. This axis looks for domain switching, self-taught projects, public writing, teaching, presentations, leadership, career transitions, and rapid upskilling.

**High-score signals:**

- Learned and applied a new technology or domain quickly
- Explained complex topics to non-technical audiences
- Created documentation, presentations, training, thought leadership, or portfolio evidence
- Reflected on failures, limitations, or improvement areas

**Low-score signals:**

- Listed experience without clarifying personal role or learning
- Limited evidence of communication, documentation, or explanation
- Little evidence of adapting to changing environments

**0-10 scoring guide:**

- 0-2: Little or no evidence of communication or learning agility
- 3-5: Some learning or communication evidence, but not very specific
- 6-8: Clear evidence of learning new problems, explaining them, and turning learning into results
- 9-10: Strong evidence of moving across domains, learning quickly, and helping others learn as well

## Six-Axis Summary

| Axis | Evaluation Focus | Key Resume Evidence |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Problem Framing and Ambiguity Handling | Ability to structure ambiguous problems | Discovery, strategy, scoping, problem definition |
| Practical Building and Execution | Ability to create working artifacts | Prototype, app, workflow, automation, deployment |
| AI Leverage and Technical Fluency | Ability to apply AI practically | LLM, RAG, agent, prompt, evaluation, AI workflow |
| Enterprise Judgment and Governance | Enterprise risk judgment | Security, privacy, compliance, MLOps, auditability |
| Stakeholder and User-Centered Thinking | Ability to balance stakeholder needs | Client-facing work, UX, workshops, cross-functional work |
| Communication, Learning Agility, and Growth | Communication and learning speed | Writing, presentation, self-taught work, domain transition |

## Fairness Design Principles

- Do not directly use school name, employer brand, age, gender, nationality, photo, address, or similar information in scoring.
- Do not automatically treat missing resume information as a weakness. If information is missing, mark it as unknown or needs follow-up.
- Design evidence types broadly so candidates from non-traditional backgrounds, self-taught paths, operations, product, UX, consulting, and other backgrounds can be evaluated fairly.
- Do not use AI scores as final decisions. Reviewers must inspect evidence and follow-up questions before making decisions.
- Each score must include evidence quotes or extracted rationale so the evaluation is auditable.
